HOT POT/FIRE POT (火锅)
(Excerpt from Wikipedia)

Hot pot is a variety of East Asian food, prepared with a simmering pot of stock at the dining table.

While the hot pot is kept simmering, ingredients are placed into the pot and are cooked at the table.

Typical hot pot dishes include thinly sliced meat, leaf vegetables, mushrooms, wontons, egg dumplings, tofu, and seafood. The cooked food is usually eaten with a dipping sauce.

Hot pot meals are usually eaten in the winter during supper time.

Project Introduction: Our team decided to create an entire Information Technology (IT) system from a planning perspective, and from the business requirements to deployment.

Problem Description section describes a problem a typical restaurant business would experience.

Systems Capabilities section describes what the entire system would be capable of accomplishing, if fully implemented in a real-world setting.

Business Benefits section describes the expected advantages the system would bring to the business.

User-goals are created after discussion with a client to discover and uncover high-level functions required by different people who may use the software.

Sometimes this step can help to understand how a customer, employee and manager may need similar functionalty, but with slightly different use-cases. In our restaurant project, a client may wish to view an item, while a server may need to check stock, a kitchen may need to update stock, and a manager might want to change the description or contents of a food item.

Use-case diagrams are excellent displays for high-level functionality, and can help non-technical stakeholders understand how one to many users can require the same functionality, but may not need other functionalities. For instance, a customer, waiter and server may need to view ordered items, but only the kitchen employees need the ability to confirm prepared items.

Use-case descriptions usually describe the non-technical aspect of the use-case, and can help to answer the overall questions about what a use-case does, without being to granular with details.

Workflow descriptions describe the flow of the system and possible branches that may occur in the workflow, such as when a payment card is declined, and the transaction is returned to ready to pay mode. This is also described in a mostly non-technical approach, as in our project. Other projects, such as laboratory software, may have a need for field-specific technical description.

Activity diagrams show the logical flow of a system, making decision points well defined and clear.

User-stories are stories written by the user, sometimes lead by informed questions from the supervising Business Analyst (BA). This is where the BA's communication and trouble-shooting skills are required.

Detailed use-cases are common and are key in understanding fine-tuned use-case concepts and can vary in length from brief to very specific. Detailed use-cases can include: Scenarios, triggering events, brief descriptions, actors, other related use-cases, stakeholders, preconditions, postconditions, flow of activities and exception conditions.

Design Class Diagrams can be created in combination with a database Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD), but are standardized using the Unified Markup Language (UML) and contain more information, in addition to what is found on a typical database diagram. Creating a Design Class Diagram also helps larger projects discover unresolved dependencies and to create more unified structures in the initial programming stages.

Gantt charts are used to understand whether or not the project is on-schedule and to better manage the project. Using Microsoft Project, task dependencies can be set, timing can be estimated and meet modern Project Management principles, such as calculating available slack-time.
