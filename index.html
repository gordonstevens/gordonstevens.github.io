Data Governance and Ethics

Data governance is another emerging field of great importance, ensuring the correct data access is given to users, and can technically change when using databases versus data warehouses or data lakes. Data warehouses are more or less a catch-all for data and sorted into structured and unstructured data, and data lakes use advanced artificial intelligence (AI) to do warehousing. Data governance is concerned with the stewardship of ethical and appropriate usage of data.

Data governance knowledge domains I'm trained in include: data architecture, data modelling and design, data storage and operations, data security, data integration and interoperability, documents and content, references and master data, data warehousing and business intelligence, meta-data, and data quality.

Data governance has some things in common with data analysis and AI tasks, such as Extract, Transform, and Load (ETL), data quality, lineage, catalouging, and metrics.

I have been trained in data governance and ethics throughout my post-secondary education, have experienced first how data governance can protect employees, the business, and entrepreneurs, safe guarding trade secrets, sales, and regulatory data.

While I enjoy the technical side of data governance, the theoretical side of ethics, and the implications of regulatory bodies upon data such as privacy and storage location of data laws.

Most of all through, I have Canadian workplace experience and plenty of training in tech and business, so I love getting to know people within an organisation, and understanding how data is created, used, and destroyed within the organisation.

Databases

I love Standard Query language (SQL) which is the language of databases. Databases are used almost everywhere, can hold the most secret information on the planet, and good database admins attain knowledge in cybersecurity to ensure database permissions are correct, firewalls block unauthorised access, perhaps writing triggers to ensure another layer of security and data assuredness. Another area of knowledge in databases is where the data is actually stored, as some countries such as Canada have laws and regulations from governing bodies on data location specifics and privacy. Deployment, back-up, redundancies, are also important.
